Industry profile · Quick-Serve Retail

The typical multi-site QSR brand runs every store on a flat network and a franchise patchwork.

Across this segment, each store is a tiny edge: a POS system, kitchen display screens, drive-thru, guest Wi-Fi, digital menu boards, security cameras, and an increasingly common back-office tablet. IT staff per store? Effectively zero. The "network team" is a handful of people supporting hundreds to thousands of sites.

  • Guest Wi-Fi shares the same flat network as the POS — one compromised tablet exposes cardholder data.
  • PCI-DSS scope is the entire store because nothing is segmented.
  • A new location takes weeks to turn up; a router swap means a truck roll.
  • SaaS apps (ordering, loyalty, labor) are slow because traffic backhauls to a distant data center.
  • Franchise vs. corporate sites run different gear with no consistent policy or visibility.

Corporate + franchise under one policy and one pane of glass

Industry profile · Global Manufacturing

The typical multinational manufacturer runs plants across many countries, modernizing into Industry 4.0.

Across this segment, each plant blends modern IT with decades-old operational technology: PLCs, SCADA, robotics, sensors, and connected machines that were never designed to touch the internet — but now feed cloud analytics and AI quality systems. The attack surface exploded the moment OT met the cloud.

  • OT and IT share infrastructure; one phished office laptop can reach the plant floor and halt production.
  • Legacy machines can't run security agents, yet now require cloud connectivity for telemetry.
  • Each country has its own compliance regime (GDPR, data residency, sector rules) and its own local carrier.
  • Third-party integrators and equipment vendors need narrow remote access — today they get VPNs into the whole network.
  • Unplanned downtime costs five to seven figures per hour, so MTTR is a board-level number.
What "good" looks like
IT ⊥ OT
Hard segmentation stops lateral movement to the plant floor

Vendors reach one machine, not the network

Discovery questions for the first call

Use these to qualify either use case and find the compelling event.
  1. How many sites, and how much IT presence does each one actually have?
  2. Is guest, IoT/OT, and production traffic segmented today — or sharing one flat network?
  3. How long does it take to turn up a new location or plant right now?
  4. How do third parties and equipment vendors get remote access today?
  5. How many security vendors and consoles is the team managing across regions?
  6. What's your mean time to detect and respond when something goes wrong?
  7. Where does your traffic break out to cloud and SaaS — locally, or backhauled?
  8. What does an hour of downtime or a failed audit actually cost the business?
Objection reopeners

Keep the conversation open, not closed.

The three objections this motion hits most. Each one is answered with a question that reopens discovery — not a rebuttal that ends it.

You'll hear
We already have SASE.
Reopen with
Most who say that have SD-WAN from one vendor and security from another. When your network and security teams look at an incident, are they looking at the same screen — or reconciling two?
You'll hear
A cutover would be too disruptive.
Reopen with
That's exactly why the blueprint is phased — assess before anything moves. What would it be worth to see your highest-risk sites mapped before a single change is made?
You'll hear
Why not just buy the software direct?
Reopen with
You could — then you own the backbone, the carriers, and the remediation. Who on your team wants to run a global network under the security platform, and at what cost?
